Abstract
This study aimed to carry out a descriptive analysis of the game actions and activity profile of professional men’s singles
pickleball. A cross-sectional design was used. Match activity profile of seven male singles pickleball matches from the
Carvana Arizona Grand Slam tournament were analysed. Study variables, such as, playing and resting time parameters,
shots per rally, type of shots, trajectory of the ball and its path and shot outcomes were studied. The mean match durations were 37.1 ± 11.6 min, with a work-to-rest ratio of 1:3.8 (mean, 0.26 ± 0.03), where the effective playing time represents 20.5% of the total playing time. Most rally durations (77.4%) lasted between 3–9 s and most resting times (76.9%)
were between 9–18 s. 53.1% of rallies had between 3–5 shots. The forehand (32.3%), the serve (19.8%) and the backhand
(19.5%) accounted for 71.6% of the shots. Cross-court play (55%) predominated over parallel (45%). Most of the shots
(99%) were performed from the baseline (53.9%) and the transition zone (45.1%). The majority of the shots were intercepted (80.2%). This information enhances understanding of singles pickleball and can contribute to performance and
training session improvements.

pickleball is a recent creation, it was actually created in
1965, in the United States, by Joel Pritchard; a congressman
from Washington State and Bill Bell, a successful businessman. [2] But clearly its popularity has increased within the
latest years, making pickleball one of the fastest-growing
sports in the USA, [3,4] specifically being massified among
older adults from the same country, due to their positive
well-being factors. [5] This leads us to the contradiction in
which its popularity does not match the number of scientific
researches done for getting a better understanding of the
sports-specific requirements, players profile and game
dynamics. For instance, in the current literature, two investigations can be found regarding technical-tactical performance indicators in single women [6] and in men doubles
category [7] that can lead into a jump start for a better understanding of the player’s game parameters at a professional

Methods


Design


An observational cross-sectional design was conducted, being
nomothetical and multidimensional, given the plurality of participants and the parameters to be analysed. These ones were
classified into various criteria and categories, which are presented in the instrument of observation (Table 1). This was
a non-participatory process due to the observer did not interact
with the subjects during the observation. [27]


Sample


With the approval of institutional ethics (2324006P), a total
of 3668 records were analysed throughout seven males
individual pickleball matches corresponding to quarterfinals (n = 4), semifinals (n = 2) and final (n = 1) of the
Carvana Arizona Grand Slam tournament held in
February 2023. Eight different professional male players
were observed (mean ± SD: age, 27.3 ± 5.4 years; height
1.86 ± 0.05 m; background as a professional, 2.6 ± 2.1
years; ranking, 9.6 ± 8.2) recognized by points of the
Professional Pickleball Association. [28] Every single player
was right-handed for racket manipulation, which does not
ensure their dominant upper-laterality.

Procedure


Three videos in live format filmed by the PPA and rebroadcast on Youtube through the PPA Tour channel were taken
as analysis material. All the observational process was computerized through the LINCE PLUS 1.3.2 software, [29] using
an ad-hoc observation instrument with specific criteria and
categories for the present study (Table 1). The observational
instrument was designed following the parameters on previous scientific publications on the analysis of the game and
activity profile of tennis, padel and men doubles pickleball [7,9,18] and the official pickleball regulation. [30] It consists
of eight criteria, which includes points duration with the
beginning and ending time, pause time between points
with the beginning and ending of the pauses, player’s
hitting location on court (Figure 1), type of shot performed,
the trajectory followed by the ball, the route of the ball after
being hit and the result of the analysed shot. The data from
each match was exported in “.csv” format which was

Data quality control


The construct validity was obtained from the consistency of
the conceptual framework by four pickleball coaches (graduated from the RFET; Real Federación Española de Tenis –
Royal Spanish Tennis Federation), who reached 100%
agreement for each of the categories. The validation was
carried out using a virtual questionnaire, where the
experts could agree or disagree with the categorisation of
each criterion. The reliability was determined after the
observers had received a period of training in the application of the registration instrument and after reaching an
agreement. [31] The reliability of the observation was determined by means of intra and inter-observer agreement in
200 game actions. A 0.91 Kappa coefficient, [32] was obtained
in the contrast between the observation carried by the main
author and the observation carried out by a racket-sports
technician. Same procedure was used for intra-observer
agreement resulting in a Kappa coefficient of 0.98.


Statistical analyses


A descriptive study of main study variables was performed.
Absolute (n) and relative (%) frequencies were computed
for categorical variables, while measures of central tendency and dispersion were calculated for the continuous
ones. For the comparison between effective playing time
and resting time (in minutes), a paired samples t-test was
conducted. The mean difference and 95% confidence intervals were calculated to provide context for the observed



differences. For the comparison between rally duration
and pause between rallies (in seconds), a non-parametric
Wilcoxon signed-rank test was used due to tied pairs and
potential non-normality in the data distribution. The
Spearman rank-order correlation coefficient for nonparametric data was used to examine the relationship
between rally duration and the number of shots per rally.
Correlation was classified as trivial (0–0.1), small (0.1–0.3),
moderate (0.3–0.5), large (0.5–0.7), very large (0.7–0.9),
nearly perfect (0.9), and perfect (1.0). [33] The significance
level was set at 0.05. The mean difference and 95% confidence
intervals were reported. The reliability was measured with the
kappa coefficient. [32] All analyses were performed using
version 4.1.3 of the R statistical software.

The forehand (32.3%; mean per match, 141.3 ± 60.5), the

Players hitting locations


Almost all of the of the shots performed (99%) were made

the kitchen, very few shots occurred (0.1%; mean shots
per match, 4.3 ± 1.9) (Table 3).

Discussion


To our knowledge, this is the first study that provides
detailed information on playing actions and activity
profile of professional males individual pickleball based
on the analysis of the characteristics of each shot and duration parameters of the points. The results showed that
the mean duration of the point was 5.3 ± 3 s interposed
with pauses of 20.7 ± 20.1 s with a total effective playing
time of 20.5%. The points mostly lasted between 3 and
9 s (77.4%) whereas pauses between 9 and 18 s (76.9%).
53.1% of the rallies consisted in 3–5 shots. The forehand
(32.3%), the serve (19.8%) and the backhand (19.5%)
accounted for 71.6% of the shots, with a predominance
on cross-line ball trajectories (55.1%). The player’s
common hitting locations were the baseline area (54%), followed by the transition zone (45.1%). According to the
International Pickleball Federation, there is a rule that
only points are added to the scoreboard when points are
won while serving, therefore, the number of points per



game can be very variable, and consequently, the number
of shots and the duration of the games as well. Hence, a
total of 607 (mean per match, 86.7 ± 29.2) points have
been analysed, in which only 283 (mean per match, 40.4
± 13) have finally hit the scoreboard.
The average points duration was 5.3 ± 3 s, lasting the
majority of the points between 3 and 6 s (51.9%),
showing a difference with the average of points duration
on men doubles category (10.7 ± 8.7 s) where the 55.4%
of the points lasted between 3 and 9 s. [7] Comparing these
results with tennis, we could see that there are higher percentage of points that end in less than 3 s, although the
average duration of the points is slightly longer than pickleball points. [8,10,34,35] Taking into consideration, in pickleball
it is very difficult to produce winning serves, while in tennis
it is much easier (aces). However, points that end as quickly
as a direct serve in tennis can also come out, because there
is no second serve, therefore, if the first serve is missed, the
point is over, although this situation is not very frequent at a
professional level. [6,7] Likewise, padel has a longer duration
including values from 7.24 ± 0.0 to 16.8 ± 2.8 s, [16] considering the frequent use of lobs and wall-bounces during the
game, which could be a reason why longer rally durations
are produced. [36] Comparing the duration of the points with
table tennis, we see how in this sport the points tend to
last less than in pickleball. [20,37] The interval of frequency
of resting time duration is quite similar to the given in
tennis, although the average pause time between points is
somewhat lower. [8] In pickleball there are pauses lasting
more than 30 s (8.8%), which usually represents the

resting time, as well as rally duration and pause between
rallies (p < .001), it is an important parameter to consider
when designing further training programmes.
Just like in pickleball men doubles (40.2%) [7] and
tennis, [9,42,43] the forehand was the most performed shot
(32%). The predominance over the backhand (19.5%),
could be because in many points, when players noticed a
centred-ball, they tended to reverse themselves for better
shot performance (skilled face of the racket). These
results differ with other racket-sports (i.e., padel) where
the most performed shots are the volleys. [18,44,45] The
backhand volley (14%) showed a higher percentage than
the forehand volley (9%) because, during a point, there
are quick interchanges from a very close distance between
players, which means a very short time interval to react,
and in these cases, the backhand volley acts as a “shield”
covering more body than the forehand volley for defending.
These results also are repeated at men doubles category,
where the backhand volley outnumbered the forehand
volleys (18.4% and 12.7% respectively). [7] There were not
many lobs (1.5%), possibly due to the court length is not
very long, therefore, whenever performed, it was a last
resource tool. And, as for the drop shots (3%), likewise as
the lobs, as the court is not long, the fact of performing
one of these shots is not usually considered as an option
to make a winning shot, rather is usually used to approach
the opponent player to the net and then generate a winning
shot or a forced error.
Regarding the player’s hitting location on court, there was
a predominance of the baseline shots (54%) over the transition (45.1%) which is the zone that follows the baseline
towards the non-volley zone. Same situation occurred at
women singles in which the baseline zone accounted more
shots performed rather than the transition (38.6% and
35.8% respectively). [6] Interesting situation happened at men
doubles, where opposite results were found in which shots
performed at the transition zone (60.7%) exceeded those performed at the baseline (15.2%). [7] This notes that players at
individual categories, intend to balance their game play prioritizing the baseline and the transition zone over the nonvolley zone; where the fewer shots are performed due to
the limitations of the rule of the non-volley zone, which
does not allow to enter this zone to hit the ball, unless it
has bounced before. Moving forward, during an attacking
phase, it seems that players are more interested in hitting
the ball in a direct way from the transition limit zone with
the non-volley line, rather than the ball bouncing inside the
non-volley zone and then hitting the ball in an indirect
way. Performing a shot in an indirect way supposes a
greater interval of time between interchange of the ball,
which gives more responding time to the opponent. On the
contrary, when a player is being dominated by the opponent,
it is in his interest to perform a shot so that it bounces inside
the contrary non-volley zone, in order to have more time to
recover the position.

As for the shot outcomes, 80.2% of the shots were intercepted, 8.7% unforced errors, 6.2% winners and 5% forced
errors. These percentages obtained are quite different from
those of tennis, where the winning shots and unforced
errors are more common. [35,42,46] In contrast, the percentages
obtained in the present study agree with those found in men
doubles category [7] and padel. [18] In relation to the ball trajectory, the fact that the cross-line (55%) game was predominant over the parallel (45%), is definitely related to the
sport-regulations, which says that serves must be performed
in a cross-line. In padel, we could find a study that makes
the same reflection regarding this aspect, in which crossline balls predominate over the parallels. [18,47] Regarding
the ball actions after being hit, the bounce predominated
(60.1%) over the non-bounced balls (24.8%). This gives us
a bigger number of indirect shots over the direct ones.
Taking into account the pickleball regulation that describes
the bounce rule, in which the serve must bounce in the opposite court before hitting, that these differences between indirect
and direct shots were totally expected.
It should be noted that the obtained results of this
research might be useful when establishing parameters
and criteria on future training sessions for players competing at single category. Regarding technical-tactical aspects,
it is primordial to consider the fact of practicing every shot
during training sessions, emphasizing the most performed
ones: forehand, backhand and serve, especially this last
one, which requires full attention as it is a “single-shot”
shot. Hitting locations are also recommended to work on,
specifically from the baseline as well as from the transition
zone and favouring the cross-court patterns. In addition, the
percentage of effective playing time, points durations and
the number of shots per rally, allows coaches and players
to approximate the workload of matches into practical sessions, which is essential for developing the player’s
performance.
This study represents an initial attempt to understand the
patterns and behaviour of professional male players in
match situations. Although the sample consists of only
eight players and seven matches, the analysis includes a
total of 3668 recorded shots, providing a detailed insight
into performance trends. Nevertheless, the findings should
not be generalised beyond this specific context. Future
studies should expand the sample to include players of different skill levels, ages, and genders, as results may vary
accordingly. Additionally, this study focused exclusively
on technical-tactical performance indicators, without considering physiological or physical demands, which would
be an interesting avenue for future research.


Conclusion


Understanding the competitive dynamics of pickleball is of
vital importance in its daily practice, and coaches should
know the characteristics of the match actions that occur



and the activity profile of the sport in which they are
involved. The mean duration of a competitive match is
about 37.1 min, with a work-to-rest ratio of 1:3.8, where
the actual playing time represents almost 21% of the total
playing time. Most of the points last between 3 and 9 s
and the pauses between 9 and 18 s. Shots per rally are commonly consisting in 3 to 5 shots. The most frequent shots in
professional males individual pickleball are the forehand,
serve and the backhand. There is a predominance of the
cross-line game over the parallel. Almost all of the shots
are performed from the baseline and the transition zone.
Intercepted shots are the most produced during a match
rather than unforced errors, winning shots and forced
errors. The majority of the ball post hitting actions are the
bounces over the non-bouncing balls which determine
more indirect shots rather than direct ones.
